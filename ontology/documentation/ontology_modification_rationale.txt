I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
================================================================================

The Hebrew Manuscripts Ontology has been developed from Prof. Gila Prebor's 
original design based on LRMoo/CIDOC-CRM. Our modifications address three 
fundamental challenges identified by the expert reviewers:

1. PHILOSOPHICAL TENSION between bibliographic-practical cataloging and new 
   philological approaches that reject the notion of a unified "Work."

2. STRUCTURAL RIGIDITY in the original BU/CU/PU hierarchy that could not 
   represent complex nested manuscripts (MTM, Sammelbands).

3. EPISTEMOLOGICAL AMBIGUITY in distinguishing observable facts from scholarly 
   interpretations, particularly important for AI-derived attributions.

These challenges required substantial additions while preserving the original 
LRMoo/CIDOC-CRM foundation, resulting in a hybrid ontology that supports 
multiple scholarly paradigms simultaneously.

================================================================================
II. GILA PREBOR'S ORIGINAL DESIGN
================================================================================

Prof. Gila Prebor's original ontology was built on established international 
standards and reflected a bibliographic-practical orientation:

FOUNDATIONAL FRAMEWORK
----------------------

1. LRMoo (Library Reference Model - object-oriented)
   The IFLA standard for bibliographic data, providing the Work-Expression-
   Manifestation-Item (WEMI) framework. This framework assumes:
   
   - Works are abstract intellectual creations with stable identity
   - Expressions are specific realizations of Works
   - Manifestations are physical embodiments
   - Items are individual copies

2. CIDOC-CRM (Conceptual Reference Model)
   The ISO standard for cultural heritage, providing:
   
   - Event-based modeling (activities, creations, transfers)
   - Temporal and spatial entities
   - Actor (person/group) modeling

3. Three-Level Unit Structure
   Prebor introduced three complementary unit types:
   
   - Bibliographic Unit (BU): The catalog record level
   - Codicological Unit (CU): Physical production units (shared quires/material)
   - Paleographical Unit (PU): Scribal hand units

STRENGTHS OF ORIGINAL DESIGN
----------------------------

1. Standards Compliance: Built on internationally recognized ontologies, 
   ensuring interoperability with library systems and Wikidata.

2. Event-Based Modeling: Properly represented manuscript history through 
   events (copying, acquisition, conservation).

3. Hebrew-Specific Extensions: Included colophon modeling, script type 
   classifications, and Hebrew language support.

4. Flexible Identification: Supported multiple identifiers (NLI, Sfardata, 
   VIAF, Wikidata).

LIMITATIONS IDENTIFIED BY REVIEWERS
-----------------------------------

1. "Clean Case" Assumption: The model worked excellently for straightforward 
   manuscripts (single text, one scribe, clear structure) but struggled with
   complex real-world cases.

2. Fixed Hierarchy: The BU→CU→PU relationship was not recursive, preventing
   adequate representation of manuscripts with sub-sections within sections.

3. Binary Certainty: No mechanism for expressing degrees of confidence in 
   attributions or distinguishing expert opinions from AI-derived data.

4. Monolithic Paradigm: Assumed bibliographic Work-based cataloging was the 
   only valid approach, not accounting for philological perspectives that
   reject this model.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
III.A. Prof. Moshe Lavi's Comments
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Prof. Lavi, a specialist in rabbinic literature and digital humanities, 
provided critical feedback that shaped the epistemological direction of 
the ontology:

COMMENT 1: CU vs. Content Unit Confusion
"הבלבול בין יחידה קודיקולוגית ליחידת תוכן - שרץ לאורך כל המסמך"

ANALYSIS: Prof. Lavi identified confusion between codicological units (physical
production units) and content units (textual divisions). The original design
conflated these in some contexts.

OUR SOLUTION: We introduced clearer separation through:
- hm:Bibliographic_Unit for catalog-level description
- hm:Codicological_Unit strictly for physical units
- hm:Paleographical_Unit for scribal hands
- Content described through lrmoo:F2_Expression

COMMENT 2: Fact vs. Interpretation (הכרעת חוקר)
"הצורך באבחנה בין נתונים שהם חד משמעיים לבין נתונים שהם תולדת הכרעת חוקר"

ANALYSIS: This was Prof. Lavi's most fundamental concern. Manuscript catalogs
contain data of radically different epistemological status:
- Direct observations: "The manuscript has 289 folios"
- Scholarly interpretations: "The manuscript was copied in Italy"
- AI derivations: "MIDRASH system identifies script as Italian, 75% confidence"

OUR SOLUTION: We introduced a comprehensive Epistemological Framework:
- hm:EpistemologicalStatus class with instances:
  - hm:DirectObservation (unambiguous facts)
  - hm:ScholarlyInterpretation (expert decisions)
  - hm:ComputationalDerivation (AI/algorithm outputs)
  - hm:CatalogInherited (unverified catalog data)
  - hm:TraditionalAttribution (long-standing attributions)

- hm:EvidenceChain class to document reasoning paths
- hm:EvidenceStep hierarchy (DirectObservationStep, InterpretationStep, 
  ComputationalStep) to trace conclusions to their foundations
- hm:DataCategory to classify data types (measurements, attributions, 
  relationships)
- hm:InterpretationMethod to specify analytical methods used

COMMENT 3: Granularity Support
"האנתולוגיה צריכה לספק תשתית למיפוי של כתב היד ברמת העמודה / השורה"

ANALYSIS: Automated tools can provide descriptions at much finer granularity
than traditional catalogs - down to word and character level.

OUR SOLUTION: We introduced full granularity support:
- hm:TextLocation class hierarchy:
  - hm:FolioLocation (page level)
  - hm:LineLocation (line level)
  - hm:WordLocation (word level)
  - hm:CharacterLocation (character level)
  - hm:TextSpan (ranges between locations)
- IIIF integration through hm:coordinates_iiif and hm:iiif_region
- Precise location properties (folio_number, line_number, etc.)

COMMENT 4: Canonical Hierarchies
"יש להצביע על לפחות 4 הירארכיות קאנוניות: המקרא, המשנה, משנה תורה, שולחן ערוך"

ANALYSIS: Jewish literature is organized according to well-defined reference
systems that manuscripts must be mapped against.

OUR SOLUTION: We introduced canonical hierarchy support:
- hm:CanonicalReference class with subclasses:
  - hm:BiblicalReference (Book/Chapter/Verse)
  - hm:MishnaicReference (Tractate/Chapter/Mishnah)
  - hm:TalmudicReference (Tractate/Folio/Side)
  - hm:HalachicReference (for Mishneh Torah/Shulchan Aruch)
- hm:CanonicalHierarchyType with instances for each major hierarchy
- hm:covers_canonical_range to link expressions to coverage
- hm:is_commentary_on_canonical for commentary relationships

III.B. Dr. Eliezer Baumgarten's Comments
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Dr. Baumgarten, working directly with manuscript digitization and the MIDRASH
project, provided practical feedback grounded in real cataloging challenges:

COMMENT 1: Certainty/Confidence Mechanism
"לא מצאתי דרך פורמלית לתעד דרגת ביטחון או ייחוס משוער"

ANALYSIS: Catalogs need to distinguish "certain" from "probable" from 
"possible" identifications. This is especially critical when AI systems 
provide attributions alongside human experts.

OUR SOLUTION: We introduced certainty mechanisms:
- hm:CertaintyLevel class with instances:
  - hm:Certain, hm:Probable, hm:Possible, hm:Uncertain
- hm:has_certainty property applicable to any assertion
- hm:certainty_percentage for numeric confidence (0-100)
- hm:certainty_note for textual explanation
- hm:AttributionSource class with instances:
  - hm:ExpertAttribution, hm:AIAttribution, hm:CatalogAttribution, etc.
- hm:attribution_source and hm:attributed_by properties

COMMENT 2: Partial Identification ("Maybe" Relationships)
"כרגע נראה שאם חיבור לא מזוהה בוודאות, פשוט נמנעים מקישורו ל־Work ספציפי"

ANALYSIS: The original design forced binary identification - either a text 
IS a specific Work or it isn't. Real manuscripts often contain texts that 
"might be" or "resemble" known works.

OUR SOLUTION: We introduced variant relationships:
- hm:is_variant_of for general variant relationship
- hm:possibly_realises for uncertain Work identification
- hm:is_abridgment_of for shortened versions
- hm:is_adaptation_of for adaptations
- hm:parallels (symmetric) for texts with shared content but unclear relation

COMMENT 3: Textual Overlap
"יחס חדש מסוג has_textual_overlap_with יכול לפתור את זה"

ANALYSIS: When multiple scribes copy overlapping portions of the same text
(as in Huntington 115), this overlap must be explicitly modeled.

OUR SOLUTION: We added:
- hm:has_textual_overlap_with (symmetric property)
- hm:duplicates_part_of for partial duplication relationships

COMMENT 4: Multi-Volume/Anthology Structure
"ההיררכיה PU–CU–BU לא עובדת כאשר יש כתבי יד שבהם CU אחת כוללת כמה יחידות משנה"

ANALYSIS: The fixed hierarchy cannot represent:
- Multi-volume sets where volumes are separate manuscripts
- Anthology manuscripts with deliberate internal organization
- Nested sections within sections

OUR SOLUTION: We addressed this comprehensively:
- hm:MultiVolumeSet class and hm:is_volume_of property
- hm:AnthologyStructure and hm:AnthologyPosition
- Recursive hm:has_sub_unit (transitive) property
- hm:CodicologicalHierarchy to manage complex structures
- hm:CompositeCodicologicalUnit and hm:AtomicCodicologicalUnit

COMMENT 5: Nested CU Structure
"אולי לאפשר קינון של CU בתוך CU"

ANALYSIS: This was Dr. Baumgarten's most significant structural suggestion.
Complex manuscripts like Oppenheimer 128 have codicological units that 
themselves contain sub-units with distinct characteristics.

OUR SOLUTION: We introduced full recursive composition:
- hm:has_sub_unit as owl:TransitiveProperty, enabling:
  CU_Main → CU_Part_A → CU_Section_1 (arbitrary depth)
- hm:is_sub_unit_of as inverse property
- hm:nesting_level to track depth
- hm:is_atomic_unit boolean to identify leaf nodes
- hm:CodicologicalHierarchy to manage the overall structure
- hm:hierarchy_type to classify complexity (Simple, Nested, Complex, 
  Fragmentary)

COMMENT 6: Scribal Interventions
"אני לא בטוח שיש תיעוד מספיק של שינויי ידיים, עיטורים, תיקונים והוספות שוליים"

OUR SOLUTION: We added:
- hm:ScribalIntervention class with subclasses:
  - hm:TextCorrection
  - hm:MarginalAddition
  - hm:HandChange
- hm:has_scribal_intervention property
- hm:intervention_location, hm:intervention_by properties
- hm:intervention_description datatype property

COMMENT 7: Lost Manuscripts
"סוגיה אחרונה היא כ״י שאבדו או שאנו מכירים רק דרך עותק אחר"

OUR SOLUTION: We added:
- hm:LostManuscript class
- hm:HypotheticalExemplar subclass
- hm:is_copy_of_lost and hm:known_through properties

III.C. Prof. Gila Prebor's Self-Reflection
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

In her response to the other experts' feedback, Prof. Prebor articulated the 
fundamental philosophical tension:

THE DILEMMA
"יש כאן מתח בין שתי גישות: הגישה שלי היא ביבליוגרפית-מעשית... הגישה של משה 
ואליעזר (פילולוגיה חדשה) היא תיאורטית-תהליכית"

ANALYSIS: Prof. Prebor identified a fundamental incompatibility:

BIBLIOGRAPHIC-PRACTICAL APPROACH (Prebor's original orientation):
- Assumes "Work" exists as identifiable abstract entity
- Works have Expressions (versions) that can be cataloged
- Suitable for Wikidata integration and library systems
- Goal: Organize information for retrieval

NEW PHILOLOGICAL APPROACH (Lavi/Baumgarten orientation):
- Rejects unified "Work" concept as anachronistic
- Each manuscript is a unique cultural event
- Textual fluidity is the norm, not deviation
- Goal: Represent transmission dynamics

THE QUESTION
"האם אפשרית גישה היברידית שמוסיפה Text Tradition כרמת ביניים?"
"או שהמתח עמוק מדי וצריך שתי אונטולוגיות נפרדות?"

OUR ANSWER: We proved a hybrid approach IS possible through the 
Dual Paradigm Framework:

- hm:ManuscriptView abstract class for paradigmatic perspectives
- hm:CatalogingView for bibliographic approach (uses Work/Expression)
- hm:PhilologicalView for new philology (uses TextTradition/Witness)
- hm:ParadigmBridge to explicitly link Work and TextTradition when both 
  apply to the same content
- Manuscripts can have both views simultaneously
- hm:is_primary_paradigm to indicate preferred approach per manuscript
- hm:paradigm_justification for scholarly documentation

This design allows:
1. Catalogers to use familiar Work-based description
2. Philologists to use tradition-based analysis
3. Explicit documentation of when/why paradigms align or differ
4. No forced choice between incompatible worldviews

IV. OUR MODIFICATIONS AND SOLUTIONS
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
IV.A. Dual Paradigm Framework
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM STATEMENT:
Prof. Prebor identified an apparently irreconcilable philosophical tension 
between two approaches to manuscript description:

1. Bibliographic Approach (suitable for Wikidata/cataloging):
   - Texts are "copies" of abstract Works
   - Work identity is stable across manuscripts
   - Variations are "deviations" from original

2. Philological Approach (suitable for research):
   - Each manuscript is a unique cultural event
   - There is no "original" - only streams of transmission
   - All witnesses are equally primary sources

DESIGN SOLUTION:
Rather than forcing a choice, the ontology supports both paradigms 
simultaneously through a "view" architecture:

hm:ManuscriptView (abstract)
├── hm:CatalogingView
│   ├── cataloging_work → lrmoo:F1_Work
│   └── cataloging_expression → lrmoo:F2_Expression
│
└── hm:PhilologicalView
    ├── philological_tradition → hm:TextTradition
    └── philological_witness → hm:TransmissionWitness

hm:ParadigmBridge
├── has_linked_work → lrmoo:F1_Work
├── has_linked_tradition → hm:TextTradition
└── paradigm_justification (text explaining relationship)

WHY THIS WORKS:
1. Neither paradigm is privileged - users choose based on purpose
2. hm:is_primary_paradigm indicates which view is dominant for a manuscript
3. ParadigmBridge explicitly documents when Work and Tradition align
4. Paradigms can coexist without contradiction
5. Each paradigm uses its own classes (Work vs. TextTradition)

PRACTICAL EXAMPLE:
The Buber Tanchuma case illustrates the need:
- Bibliographic view: This manuscript is an Expression of "Tanchuma Midrash"
- Philological view: This manuscript witnesses the "Buber Tradition" which
  developed independently and is not a "copy" of a unified original

With dual paradigm support:
:MS_Buber_Tanchuma 
    hm:has_cataloging_perspective :CatView1 ;
    hm:has_philological_perspective :PhilView1 .

:CatView1 a hm:CatalogingView ;
    hm:cataloging_work :Work_Midrash_Tanchuma ;
    hm:is_primary_paradigm false .

:PhilView1 a hm:PhilologicalView ;
    hm:philological_tradition :Tradition_Tanchuma_Buber ;
    hm:is_primary_paradigm true ;
    hm:paradigm_note "Buber version represents distinct tradition stream" .

IV.B. Nested Codicological Structure
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM STATEMENT:
The original BU→CU→PU hierarchy was flat. Dr. Baumgarten identified cases 
where this fails:

Oppenheimer 128: A CU that contains multiple sub-CUs with distinct origins
Parma 3122: An anthology with nested organizational structure
Vatican 44: Multi-volume set with complex internal structure

The hierarchy could not represent: CU → sub-CU → sub-sub-CU

DESIGN SOLUTION:
Rather than creating parallel hierarchies for different depths, we made 
the hierarchy recursive through a transitive property:

hm:has_sub_unit rdf:type owl:ObjectProperty , owl:TransitiveProperty ;
    rdfs:domain hm:Codicological_Unit ;
    rdfs:range hm:Codicological_Unit .

This means:
- Any CU can contain sub-CUs
- Those sub-CUs can contain their own sub-CUs
- The relationship is transitive: if A has_sub_unit B and B has_sub_unit C,
  then A has_sub_unit C is inferred
- No limit on nesting depth

SUPPORTING CLASSES:

hm:CodicologicalHierarchy
- Describes the overall structure of a manuscript
- Links to hierarchy_root (top-level CU)
- Has hierarchy_type (Simple, Nested, Complex, Fragmentary)
- Tracks max_nesting_depth

hm:CompositeCodicologicalUnit (subClassOf hm:Codicological_Unit)
- Parent units that contain sub-units

hm:AtomicCodicologicalUnit (subClassOf hm:Codicological_Unit)
- Leaf-level units with no sub-divisions

DATATYPE PROPERTIES FOR NAVIGATION:
- hm:nesting_level (integer, 0 = root)
- hm:is_atomic_unit (boolean)
- hm:unit_sequence (ordering among siblings)

PRACTICAL EXAMPLE:
Representing a complex Sammelband:

:MS_Complex_Sammelband a lrmoo:F4_Manifestation_Singleton ;
    hm:has_hierarchy :Hierarchy_Complex .

:Hierarchy_Complex a hm:CodicologicalHierarchy ;
    hm:hierarchy_type hm:ComplexHierarchy ;
    hm:hierarchy_root :CU_Binding_Unit ;
    hm:max_nesting_depth 3 ;
    hm:hierarchy_description "19th century binding of 3 medieval MSS" .

:CU_Binding_Unit a hm:CompositeCodicologicalUnit ;
    hm:nesting_level 0 ;
    hm:has_sub_unit :CU_Medieval_MS_1, :CU_Medieval_MS_2, :CU_Medieval_MS_3 .

:CU_Medieval_MS_1 a hm:CompositeCodicologicalUnit ;
    hm:nesting_level 1 ;
    hm:unit_sequence 1 ;
    hm:has_sub_unit :CU_Quires_1_3, :CU_Quires_4_6 .

:CU_Quires_1_3 a hm:AtomicCodicologicalUnit ;
    hm:nesting_level 2 ;
    hm:is_atomic_unit true ;
    hm:has_folio_range "1r-24v" .

IV.C. Epistemological Framework
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PROBLEM STATEMENT:
Prof. Lavi's fundamental concern: "הצורך באבחנה בין נתונים שהם חד משמעיים 
לבין נתונים שהם תולדת הכרעת חוקר"

Manuscript data falls into distinct epistemological categories:

TYPE 1: DIRECT OBSERVATIONS (Facts)
- "The manuscript has 289 folios" (count them)
- "The colophon says 'Written by Yosef ben David'" (transcription)
- "The dimensions are 320 x 235 mm" (measurement)
- These are verifiable, unambiguous, objective

TYPE 2: SCHOLARLY INTERPRETATIONS
- "The manuscript was copied in Italy" (based on script analysis)
- "The date is approximately 14th century" (paleographic dating)
- "This text is Maimonides' Mishneh Torah" (identification)
- These require expert judgment and may be contested

TYPE 3: COMPUTATIONAL DERIVATIONS
- "MIDRASH system identifies Ashkenazi script with 78% confidence"
- "HTR transcription of folio 15r"
- "Automatic watermark matching suggests Venice 1420-1440"
- These are algorithm outputs requiring distinct validation

TYPE 4: INHERITED CATALOG DATA
- Data from existing catalogs without independent verification
- May perpetuate errors or outdated scholarship

DESIGN SOLUTION:

Core Classification:
hm:EpistemologicalStatus class with instances:
- hm:DirectObservation
- hm:ScholarlyInterpretation
- hm:ComputationalDerivation
- hm:CatalogInherited
- hm:TraditionalAttribution

Applied via: hm:has_epistemological_status (domain: owl:Thing)

Quick Filter:
hm:is_factual (boolean) - true for direct observations

Evidence Documentation:
hm:EvidenceChain - documents reasoning from data to conclusion
hm:EvidenceStep hierarchy:
- DirectObservationStep (raw data)
- InterpretationStep (expert analysis)
- ComputationalStep (algorithm output)

Each step can have:
- evidence_source (what document/data)
- evidence_agent (who/what provided it)
- interpretation_method (paleographic, codicological, AI-based, etc.)
- evidence_strength (0-1 numeric)
- consensus_level (unanimous, majority, disputed, controversial)
- derives_from (link to prior step)
- competing_interpretation (alternative analyses)

Data Classification:
hm:DataCategory class with instances:
- hm:PhysicalMeasurement (definitely factual)
- hm:ColophonContent (factual transcription, interpretation of meaning separate)
- hm:MaterialObservation (factual)
- hm:DateAttribution (usually interpretive)
- hm:AuthorshipAttribution (interpretive)
- hm:ScribeIdentification (interpretive)
- hm:PlaceAttribution (interpretive)
- hm:WorkIdentification (interpretive)
- hm:TextualRelationship (interpretive)

Method Tracking:
hm:InterpretationMethod with instances:
- hm:PaleographicAnalysis
- hm:CodicologicalAnalysis
- hm:LinguisticAnalysis
- hm:HistoricalContextAnalysis
- hm:ComparativeTextualAnalysis
- hm:AIBasedAnalysis
- hm:RadiocarbonDating
- hm:WatermarkAnalysis

PRACTICAL EXAMPLE:
Documenting a dating attribution:

:Dating_Vatican_44 a owl:Thing ;
    hm:has_epistemological_status hm:ScholarlyInterpretation ;
    hm:is_factual false ;
    hm:data_category hm:DateAttribution ;
    hm:has_certainty hm:Probable ;
    hm:has_evidence_chain :Chain_Dating_Vat44 .

:Chain_Dating_Vat44 a hm:EvidenceChain ;
    hm:supports_assertion :Dating_Vatican_44 ;
    hm:evidence_step :Step1_Script_Obs, :Step2_Paleographic, :Step3_Date_Conclusion .

:Step1_Script_Obs a hm:DirectObservationStep ;
    hm:reasoning_text "Square Italian script observed throughout" ;
    hm:evidence_strength 1.0 ;
    hm:evidence_agent :Cataloger_2025 .

:Step2_Paleographic a hm:InterpretationStep ;
    hm:interpretation_method hm:PaleographicAnalysis ;
    hm:derives_from :Step1_Script_Obs ;
    hm:reasoning_text "Script characteristics match 14th-15th century Italian styles" ;
    hm:evidence_strength 0.85 ;
    hm:consensus_level "majority" ;
    hm:methodology_reference "Beit-Arie, Specimens of Medieval Hebrew Scripts" .

:Step3_Date_Conclusion a hm:InterpretationStep ;
    hm:derives_from :Step2_Paleographic ;
    hm:reasoning_text "Date range: 1350-1450 CE" ;
    hm:evidence_agent :Prof_Expert ;
    hm:consensus_level "majority" .

VI. THEORETICAL IMPLICATIONS
================================================================================

The development of this ontology has broader implications for digital humanities
and manuscript studies:

1. PARADIGM COEXISTENCE
   The Dual Paradigm Framework demonstrates that incompatible theoretical 
   approaches can coexist in a single ontology through explicit "view" 
   modeling. This has applications beyond manuscripts - any domain where
   multiple scholarly paradigms compete.

2. EPISTEMOLOGICAL TRANSPARENCY
   The Epistemological Framework provides a model for distinguishing facts
   from interpretations that is increasingly important as AI systems generate
   data that appears authoritative but requires distinct validation.

3. RECURSIVE HIERARCHIES
   The nested CU solution using transitive properties shows how flat 
   hierarchies can be made recursive without restructuring the entire model.

4. STANDARDS PRESERVATION
   Despite substantial additions, the ontology preserves its LRMoo/CIDOC-CRM
   foundation, maintaining compatibility with library standards and Wikidata.

================================================================================
VII. CONCLUSION
================================================================================

The Hebrew Manuscripts Ontology was developed from Prof. Gila Prebor's original 
LRMoo/CIDOC-CRM-based design to comprehensively address all expert feedback. 
The key modifications address:

1. PHILOSOPHICAL TENSION: Resolved through the Dual Paradigm Framework,
   allowing bibliographic and philological approaches to coexist.

2. STRUCTURAL RIGIDITY: Resolved through recursive hm:has_sub_unit property
   and CodicologicalHierarchy management classes.

3. EPISTEMOLOGICAL AMBIGUITY: Resolved through comprehensive classification
   of data status, evidence chains, and interpretation methods.

The v1.5 update (February 2026) adds comprehensive administrative and access
metadata coverage, ensuring complete representation of all semantically
meaningful MARC fields from NLI records. This was motivated by the need to
preserve institutional, rights, and access information that is critical for
practical manuscript research and digital humanities workflows.

v1.5 ADDITIONS:
- 4 new classes: PhysicalHolding, DigitalAccess, UsageRestriction, 
  RightsDetermination
- 6 new object properties: has_physical_holding, has_digital_access,
  has_usage_restriction, has_rights_determination, has_related_material,
  held_at
- 16 new datatype properties for holdings, access, rights, copyright,
  acquisition, and collection metadata

These additions address MARC fields that were previously considered purely
administrative but carry significant semantic value for researchers:
- Field 544 (related materials) enables discovery of related copies/fragments
- Field 597 (copyright notices) preserves institutional attribution
- Field 939 (usage restrictions) documents access policies
- Field 952 (rights determination) captures domain/contract status
- AVA fields capture physical holding details at multiple institutions
- AVE fields capture digital access points including IIIF endpoints
- Field 541 (acquisition source) preserves donor/provenance information
- Field 966 (collection name) enables collection-level queries

The final ontology contains:
- 98+ classes
- 126+ object properties
- 93+ datatype properties
- 119+ controlled vocabulary instances

It successfully supports:
- Standard library cataloging (Wikidata-compatible)
- Advanced philological research
- AI-derived data with proper provenance
- Complex manuscript structures of arbitrary depth
- All canonical Jewish text reference systems
- Full granularity from manuscript to character level
- Complete administrative and access metadata from NLI MARC records

The ontology demonstrates that with careful design, theoretical tensions can
be resolved through explicit representation rather than forced choice.
